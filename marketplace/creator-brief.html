Section 2 of 6 · The Problem

What problem does your staff solve?

The most important section. Hirers hire because they have a specific, painful problem. Describe it precisely.

Be specific about the consequence of the problem not being solved. What goes wrong? What does it cost?
See how Iris answered this +
Independent P&C brokers operate across multiple states. Each state's Department of Insurance publishes bulletins, circulars, and appetite changes — sometimes daily. Monitoring 50+ DOI websites manually is impossible for a small broker. When they miss a bulletin that affects their clients' coverage, they face E&O exposure, client complaints, and potential regulatory action. The cost of a missed bulletin can be catastrophic; the cost of monitoring is currently a full-time job.
Job title + company type + size. Not "anyone in insurance" — a specific buyer profile.
See how Iris answered this +
Independent P&C brokers and MGAs with 5–50 staff, operating across 3+ states, without a dedicated compliance function. Typically the principal or compliance manager is the buyer.
Describe the exact manual workflow being replaced. Hours per week, who does it, what tools they use. The more specific, the better.
See how Iris answered this +
A compliance analyst (or the principal themselves) spending 2–4 hours every Monday morning: (1) visiting each state DOI website, (2) reading every new bulletin, (3) deciding if it affects any client, (4) drafting and sending individual emails. At a $75/hour loaded cost, this is $150–300 per week — $7,800–$15,600 per year — for a task that Iris does continuously and automatically.
Section 3 of 6 · What It Produces

Every output must be named, formatted, triggered, and scheduled. If you cannot name it, it is not ready. This is Gate 2 of The Bar.

Format: Name — type, trigger/schedule, description. List every output. If a hirer asks "what will I receive?" — this is your answer.
See how Iris answered this +
1. Regulatory Alert — email + Slack, triggered within 2 hours of bulletin publication, for any bulletin scored 6+
2. Weekly Digest — PDF, every Monday 8am, all bulletins from prior 7 days with impact scores
3. Impact Score — 1–10 rating (1=informational, 10=immediate action required), attached to every alert
4. Monthly Trend Report — PDF, first Monday of month, regulatory environment analysis by state
5. Client Exposure Map — updated quarterly, maps each client's coverage to relevant regulatory changes
Autonomous staff sends outputs directly. Supervised staff presents outputs for the hirer to review and approve before sending.
See how Iris answered this +
Autonomous. Iris sends alerts directly — the value is speed. A regulatory alert that waits for human approval may arrive after the deadline. The hirer sets the impact score threshold (default: 6+) above which alerts are sent automatically.

Section 5 of 6 · Quality & Reliability

How does your staff handle failure?

Gates 4 and 5 of The Bar. Every AI staff fails sometimes. What matters is what happens when it does.

Three specific scenarios. Each with a specific, named response. Vague answers ("handles gracefully") fail Gate 4.
See how Iris answered this +
1. DOI website unavailable: Retries every 2 hours for 24 hours. If still unavailable, flags in the next digest as "Pending — [State] DOI website unavailable. Manual check recommended." Never silently skips.
2. Bulletin language is ambiguous (could be significant or routine): Scores conservatively at 7, adds note "Ambiguous — recommend manual review," and includes full bulletin text in alert.
3. Client book CSV format changes: Detects format mismatch on next upload, pauses client-specific scoring, sends "Action required: client book format has changed" notification with instructions.
The feedback mechanism, corpus structure, and carry-forward logic. This is Gate 5 — the calibration architecture.
See how Iris answered this +
Feedback loop: Each alert includes a one-click "Relevant / Not relevant" rating. Ratings adjust the impact scoring model for that hirer's specific state mix and client book.
Corpus: Monthly update with new bulletin types, regulatory patterns, and state-specific terminology. Iris learns the language of each state's DOI.
Carry-forward: Hirer preferences (alert threshold, notification channels, client book structure) persist across sessions. State-specific scoring adjustments accumulate over time.
